NYC Affordable Housing Production Analysis

Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) — NYC Open Data

2014 – Present
Executive Summary

This analysis examines HPD's Affordable Housing Production by Building dataset (8,983 rows) to evaluate whether housing production aligns with the goals of two mayoral plans: the Housing New York Plan (2014–2021) and Housing Our Neighbors: A Blueprint for Housing & Homelessness (2022–present). Findings reveal persistent geographic inequities across boroughs, a citywide shift toward smaller unit types, and a growing pipeline of incomplete projects under the newer plan. Note: stakeholder role is assumed for analytical purposes — this team is not contracted with HPD.

Key Findings

Bronx, Brooklyn, and Manhattan are the top three boroughs under both plans. Queens and Staten Island consistently rank at the bottom — Queens' activity is largely limited to new construction.

1-BR and 2-BR units dominate production (111,271 and 101,827 respectively). Larger family-sized units (4-BR+) are extremely scarce citywide.

Preservation is the dominant strategy in Manhattan; the Bronx and Brooklyn show a more balanced mix of new construction and preservation.

~14.7% of preservation projects (1,319 rows) were Extended Affordability Only — no physical construction occurred, only a regulatory agreement was secured.

Smaller projects complete in 1–2 years on average; larger developments average ~4 years with greater variability in timeline.

Recommendations
1

HPD should prioritize targeted investment in Queens and Staten Island, which show persistent underproduction despite Queens having demonstrated new construction capacity.

2

Incentives, zoning adjustments, or targeted programs are needed to grow the supply of 3-BR+ units and better serve larger households currently underserved by both plans.

3

HPD should separately track and report Extended Affordability Only projects to more accurately reflect the physical impact of preservation activity on housing conditions.

4

The Bronx led production under Housing New York but shows declining completions under Housing Our Neighbors — HPD should investigate whether project pipeline stagnation requires intervention.
